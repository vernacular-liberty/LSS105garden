Psychological Benefits


    • Community Gardens provide endless opportunities to reduce stress and increase positive and productive mental states. Modern living often prevents people from incorporating nature into daily life which increases “morbid rumination”, or anxious thoughts. Individuals able to take part in peaceful natural settings show decreased blood flow to the subgenual prefrontal cortex: the portion of the brain connected to negative thought patterns and emotional regulation (Reynolds). Additionally, research shows that aerobic exercise including gardening increases blood flow to the brain which reduces anxiety and depression and boosts mood and motivation (Sharma). Community gardeners report the highest levels of wellbeing in comparison to both non gardeners and solitary gardeners; This fact suggests that the community component to gardening is just as important to mental health as experiencing nature, and exercise (Koay). Although Smith College does not provide statistics on rates of diagnosed anxiety and depression, national statistics indicate that roughly 25% of people suffer from a mental illness (Gaffney).


    • Increasingly psychologists are turning towards positive psychology to find human behaviors like community gardening that protect and strengthen the mind to better withstand the mental hardships of living in contemporary (Cherry). Smith should also pivot their support for mental health towards enrichment.
